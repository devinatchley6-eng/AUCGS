AUF — Atchley Unified Field

Structural cognition state space for governed adaptive intelligence

The Atchley Unified Field (AUF) is the core structural state representation of the Atchley Unified METIS Framework (AUM). It provides the formal space in which adaptive cognition is modeled, measured, constrained, and stabilized. Rather than treating intelligence as a sequence of outputs alone, AUF represents the system as a point evolving through a compact metric state space whose coordinates encode the geometry, coherence, entropy, resilience, governance condition, computational burden, and temporal alignment of reasoning. This makes safety a property of the system’s internal structural dynamics, not merely a post hoc judgment on external behavior.


---

1. Purpose

AUF exists to solve a fundamental problem in AI safety:

Behavioral outputs are too late to be the first line of control.

A system can remain behaviorally plausible while internally drifting toward instability, collapse, incoherence, or unsafe action selection. AUF addresses this by defining a state geometry of cognition in which safety-relevant structural properties are explicitly measurable and governable.

The AUF enables:

structural monitoring of cognition

metric-based safety certification

Lyapunov and barrier-function analysis

runtime intervention and recovery

cross-module coordination with RCCL, SDE, Deepfreeze, and the Gate Graph

2. Formal State Definition

At time , the AUF structural state is

\Sigma_t = (\kappa_t,\mathcal{E}_t,H_t,R_t,\Phi_t,G_t,\Theta_t)

with components:

Symbol	Name	Domain	Meaning

	Representation curvature		geometric turning of the embedding trajectory
	Compute load		normalized runtime / inference burden
	Structural entropy		normalized entropy of the active belief / representational distribution
	Resilience		alignment of current dynamics with stable reference dynamics
	Semantic coherence		consistency of current representation with reference meaning
	Governance score		most recent admissibility / gate condition
	Temporal alignment		co-motion time deviation or temporal phase mismatch


The state space is defined as

\mathcal{M} = [-1,1] \times [0,1]^5 \times [-1,1] \subset \mathbb{R}^7

equipped with weighted metric

d_W(\Sigma,\Sigma') = \left(\sum_{i=1}^{7} w_i(\Sigma_i-\Sigma_i')^2\right)^{1/2}

where  has strictly positive weights.

Because  is closed and bounded in finite-dimensional Euclidean space, it is compact and complete. That matters because compactness gives you existence of extrema, continuity control, projection properties, and convergence guarantees used later by the invariant, Lyapunov, and Deepfreeze machinery.

3. Interpretation of the Seven Fields

3.1 Curvature 

 captures how sharply the internal representational trajectory is turning. High-magnitude curvature indicates abrupt changes in semantic direction, topic shifts, adversarial perturbation, or unstable regime transition. In the dissertation prototype, curvature is described using a normalized Menger-curvature style construction on consecutive embedding centroids. This makes it a structural signal of representational instability rather than a superficial text-level feature. 

Operationally:

low  suggests steady trajectory evolution

high  suggests sharp representational turning

sustained high curvature is treated as a precursor to drift or instability


3.2 Compute Load 

 measures normalized system burden. This variable captures the idea that safety is not independent of computational stress. Systems under excessive load can exhibit degraded responsiveness, unstable search behavior, brittle compression, or poor governance compliance.

Operationally:

low  means the system is operating within comfortable compute bounds

high  means the system is near overload and therefore closer to unsafe operating conditions


3.3 Structural Entropy 

 is the normalized entropy of the active belief or representational distribution. It tracks diversity of admissible internal structure. Low entropy corresponds to collapse into brittle or degenerate cognitive states; high entropy corresponds to greater structural diversity.

This variable anchors one of the central AUM principles: the entropy floor. The framework explicitly maintains a strictly positive lower bound , preventing collapse to overcompressed, unrecoverable, or dogmatic internal states. 

3.4 Resilience 

 measures recovery-oriented alignment of present system dynamics with a stable reference dynamic. In the dissertation, resilience is linked to cosine similarity of current and reference gradient directions. This turns resilience into a dynamical quantity rather than a vague descriptive label. 

Operationally:

high  means the system’s updates align with stabilizing directions

low  means the system is moving in a way that weakens recoverability


3.5 Semantic Coherence 

 measures how semantically aligned the current internal representation remains relative to a reference or intended task manifold. It is a direct structural counterweight to semantic drift.

Operationally:

high  means strong continuity with intended semantics

low  means increasing drift away from task-grounded meaning


3.6 Governance Score 

 encodes the latest governance state of the system. It reflects whether current reasoning and proposed action lie inside the allowable operational regime enforced by the gate system. In the demo code, governance behaves as a coarse value such as deny / constrain / pass; in the broader framework it functions as a structural coordinate coupling state evolution to runtime governance. 

3.7 Temporal Alignment 

 represents alignment with the co-motion temporal regulator. This variable tracks whether reasoning dynamics are evolving at the correct effective temporal scale relative to safety-preserving control. It couples AUF to your co-motion/time layer and makes temporal distortion a first-class structural safety signal rather than an afterthought.

5. Safe Region and Structural Admissibility

Safety in AUF is not defined behaviorally. It is defined geometrically.

A scalar invariant

\mathfrak{A} : \mathcal{M} \to \mathbb{R}

defines the safe structural region

\mathcal{S}^* = \{\Sigma \in \mathcal{M} : \mathfrak{A}(\Sigma) \ge 0\}

The interpretation is direct:

if , the current structural state is admissible

if , the system has entered a structurally unsafe regime


In the prototype demo, the invariant is implemented as the minimum of six component margins:

\mathfrak{A}(\Sigma)
=
\min\{
1-|\kappa|,
R,
\Phi,
H-H_{\min},
G,
1-\mathcal{E}
\}

which means the worst current structural constraint defines admissibility. This is a strong engineering choice because it forces safety to be bottleneck-aware: one critical failure mode is enough to trigger governance response.

6. AUF as a Metric Safety Space

The weighted metric  is not decorative. It gives the framework a quantitative way to express:

distance between cognitive states

distance to the safe set

stability decay

convergence under intervention

sensitivity of the system to structural perturbation


The natural Lyapunov function is

V(\Sigma)=d_W(\Sigma,\mathcal{S}^*)^2

This quantity is zero exactly on the safe set and positive outside it. Because it measures squared distance to structural admissibility, it has a clean operational meaning: how far the system is from safety.

The dissertation prototype explicitly ties this function to geometric convergence under the safety filter and Deepfreeze operator.

9. Relationship to Deepfreeze

Deepfreeze acts on AUF states:

\mathfrak{D} : \mathcal{M}\to\mathcal{M}

Its purpose is to contract unsafe states toward . That only works because AUF gives a geometric space and metric in which “toward safety” is mathematically well-defined.

In other words:

AUF defines the geometry

the invariant defines admissibility

Deepfreeze defines the recovery map


The dissertation’s convergence statement is of the form

d_W(\mathfrak{D}^n(\Sigma_0),\mathcal{S}^*) \le c^n d_W(\Sigma_0,\mathcal{S}^*)
\quad\text{for }0<c<1

which is a contraction-toward-a-set result rather than a global fixed-point claim.

11. Design Principles of AUF

The AUF is built around several design commitments.

11.1 Compactness

The state space is bounded and closed, enabling complete metric analysis and stable numerical governance.

11.2 Structural measurability

Each coordinate corresponds to a measurable safety-relevant structural property.

11.3 Cross-module interoperability

AUF is designed so that control theory, Bayesian updating, risk scoring, and cryptographic logging all speak through the same state representation.

11.4 Safety as geometry

Safety is encoded in the structure of state space rather than only in external rule enforcement.

11.5 Recovery compatibility

Unsafe states are not treated as terminal by default; they are states with positive distance from admissibility and are therefore reachable by recovery operators.

12. Minimal Engineering Representation

A minimal implementation can represent AUF as:

Sigma_t = {
    "kappa": float,    # [-1,1]
    "epsilon": float,  # [0,1]
    "H": float,        # [0,1]
    "R": float,        # [0,1]
    "Phi": float,      # [0,1]
    "G": float,        # [0,1]
    "Theta": float     # [-1,1]
}

A prototype invariant function can be written as:

def invariant(s, H_min=0.20):
    g1 = 1 - abs(s["kappa"])
    g2 = s["R"]
    g3 = s["Phi"]
    g4 = s["H"] - H_min
    g5 = s["G"]
    g6 = 1 - s["epsilon"]
    return min(g1, g2, g3, g4, g5, g6)

This mirrors the dissertation demo logic and gives the repo a transparent bridge between theory and implementation.

The Atchley Unified Field is the formal structural cognition space underlying the AUM framework. It represents adaptive intelligence as a state in a compact metric geometry whose coordinates measure curvature, load, entropy, resilience, coherence, governance, and temporal alignment. In the AUM architecture, AUF is the base layer from which all higher-order governance mechanisms derive their formal meaning.
